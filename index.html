Four-fifths of the Tenerife fire burned inside protected land

In August 2023 a fire above Arafo burned for eight days across north-east Tenerife. Measured from orbit, 10,628 of the 13,470 hectares it covered lay within protected boundaries — among them El Teide and Corona Forestal.

Sanju Sajimon Sentinel-2 · Sentinel-1 · VIIRS Open data only

What the satellite saw, and when

Eighteen overpasses in eight days. Bar height is the number of active-fire detections recorded on that pass. The spaces between are hours nobody was watching.

The fire was first seen from space at 02:23 UTC on 16 August 2023, as a single warm pixel on the slope above Arafo. Over the following week the VIIRS instrument passed overhead eighteen times and logged 1,026 detections in all, the last on 23 August. By then the front had travelled just under fifteen kilometres north-west, into the pine forest that covers the spine of the island.

Those detections are not a map of the fire. They are a record of moments when a satellite happened to be overhead and the ground below was hot enough to register at 375-metre resolution. To find out what the fire actually did to the land, you have to wait for the smoke to clear and look again in visible and infrared light.

That second look is what this piece measures. Comparing Sentinel-2 imagery from before the fire with imagery from after it, the burned area comes to 13,470 hectares. Of that, 10,628 hectares — 78.9 per cent — falls inside the boundaries of protected areas.

The fire did not stray onto protected land. Protected land is where almost all of it burned.

What burned

Mostly forest, and mostly not severely

Burn severity is estimated from the difference in the Normalized Burn Ratio between the two dates — how much the vegetation's reflectance changed. Two-thirds of the burned area falls in the two lower severity classes. High severity, where the canopy was consumed rather than scorched, accounts for 1,823 hectares.

By land cover, the burn was overwhelmingly wooded. Tree cover accounts for 8,543 hectares, close to two-thirds of the total. Built-up land inside the burned area comes to 182 hectares — a figure that describes what the fire's footprint overlapped, not what was damaged.

Protected land

Ten designated areas fall inside the burn

Overlaying the burned area on the World Database on Protected Areas, which includes the EU's Natura 2000 network, gives 10,628 hectares inside protected boundaries. Ten designated areas intersect the burn: El Teide, Corona Forestal, Las Lagunetas, Barranco de Fasnia y Güímar, Montaña de los Frailes, Siete Lomas, La Resbala, Campeches, Tigaiga y Ruiz, Costa de Acentejo and Rambla de Castro.

One word matters here. This figure is exposure — burned ground that sits inside a protected boundary. It is a geometric overlap between two maps. It says nothing about ecological loss, about which habitats will recover, or about how quickly. Establishing that takes fieldwork, and fieldwork is not what a satellite does.

How we measured this

And how the number checks out

Burn severity comes from Sentinel-2 optical imagery: a cloud-masked composite of six scenes from 10 July to 13 August, compared with five scenes from 12 September to 5 October, once the smoke had gone. The difference in the Normalized Burn Ratio is classified using the standard USGS thresholds, with a relativised burn ratio reported alongside it. Land cover comes from ESA WorldCover, protected boundaries from the WDPA, and the timeline from NASA FIRMS. Every dataset is open.

The area figure can be checked. Copernicus Emergency Management Service activated rapid mapping for this fire and published its own delineation.

The gap is expected and runs in the direction you would predict. A spectral index picks up lightly scorched ground at the edges that a very-high-resolution delineation draws outside the line. Agreement within ten per cent of the official product is what gives the rest of these numbers their footing.

A Sentinel-1 radar cross-check was also run, on the reasoning that radar sees through smoke where optical sensors cannot. Over this terrain the backscatter change across the scar fell below the threshold set for confirmation, so the check is recorded as inconclusive rather than quietly dropped.

The gaps

What this cannot tell you

  • The differenced Normalized Burn Ratio estimates severity from reflectance. It is not a field survey and not a legal perimeter.
  • A single post-fire composite was used. Low-severity edges make the mapped area larger than a very-high-resolution delineation would.
  • Trade-wind cloud leaves holes in the imagery over Tenerife's northern slopes. Masked pixels are shown as absent, never filled in with an estimate.
  • The Sentinel-1 cross-check is qualitative confirmation only, and here it was inconclusive. It is not a second measurement of area.
  • FIRMS detections are roughly 375 metres across. The ignition point is the first pixel the satellite detected, not the exact place the fire started.
  • The protected-area figure is exposure, not ecological damage. Nothing here counts casualties, evacuations, or buildings lost, because none of that was measured.

Where the archive runs out

The 2012 fires sit in a hole in the open record: Sentinel-2 did not exist, Landsat 5 had stopped acquiring, and Landsat 7 was returning striped imagery. Some events cannot be measured this way, and saying so is part of the method.

On the writing

A language model produced the first draft from the measurements alone — it never saw an image and computed nothing. An automated check then confirms that every figure in this piece traces back to the measured data, and rejects the draft if one does not. The text was edited and is published by a human.
